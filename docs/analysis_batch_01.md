## 1. समाधि — samādhi

**"A complete placing-together"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Root** | √dhā — "to place, put, hold" |
| **Prefixes** | sam- ("together, completely") + ā- ("toward") |
| **Suffix** | -i (action noun) |
| **Segmentation** | sam + ā + √dhā + -i |
| **Confidence** | High |

### What It Means

Built from its parts, samādhi literally means "a complete placing-together" — a total composing or settling of awareness. In Patañjali's Yoga Sutras, it is the eighth and final limb of yoga, the state where the meditator's awareness merges so fully with the object of meditation that the distinction between knower, knowing, and known dissolves.

Patañjali distinguishes two broad types: samādhi with cognitive content (samprajñāta, YS 1.17) and objectless samādhi (asamprajñāta, YS 1.18). The entire system moves toward samādhi as the condition in which liberating wisdom (prajñā) arises.

In Buddhism, samādhi refers more broadly to meditative concentration and is the last element of the Noble Eightfold Path.

### Key Sutras

- **YS 1.20**: samādhi listed among the prerequisites for higher attainment
- **YS 3.3**: "When that same [meditation] shines forth as the object alone, as if empty of its own form — that is samādhi"

### Chinese Equivalents

| Chinese | Pinyin | Type | Notes |
|---------|--------|------|-------|
| 三摩地 | sānmódì | Phonetic | Xuanzang's standardized rendering (7th c.). Syllable-by-syllable match. |
| 三昧 | sānmèi | Phonetic | Older, more common rendering. From Prakrit (loss of final -dhi). |

### Note on a Common Misreading

Some modern teachers gloss samādhi as "equal-placing" (from sama + ādhi). This folk etymology is not supported by standard Sanskrit grammar or dictionaries. The derivation is sam + ā + √dhā, not sama + ādhi.

## 2. ध्यान — dhyāna

**"The act of contemplating"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Root** | √dhyai — "to think, contemplate, meditate upon" |
| **Prefixes** | (none) |
| **Suffix** | -ana (action/process noun) |
| **Segmentation** | √dhyai + -ana |
| **Confidence** | High |

### What It Means

Dhyāna is the seventh limb — the sustained, unbroken stream of attention toward a single object. It follows concentration (dhāraṇā) and precedes absorption (samādhi). Patañjali defines it precisely: "the continuous flow of a single cognition" (pratyayaikatānatā, YS 3.2).

In Buddhist practice, dhyāna (Pali: jhāna) refers to four progressive stages of meditative absorption, each defined by which mental factors are present or absent.

### Why This Term Matters Beyond Yoga

The word dhyāna is one of the most consequential loanwords in Asian religious history:

> Sanskrit **dhyāna** → Pali **jhāna** → Chinese **禪那** (chánnà) → abbreviated **禪** (chán) → Japanese **禅** (zen)

An entire school of Buddhism — Chan/Zen — takes its name from this single Sanskrit word.

### Key Sutra

- **YS 3.2**: "There [in the place of dhāraṇā], the continuous flow of a single cognition is dhyāna"

### Chinese Equivalents

| Chinese | Pinyin | Type | Notes |
|---------|--------|------|-------|
| 禪那 | chánnà | Phonetic | Full transliteration of dhyāna (via Prakrit jhāna). |
| 禪 | chán | Phonetic | Abbreviated form. Became the name of Chan Buddhism. |
| 禪定 | chándìng | Hybrid | 禪 (phonetic) + 定 ("settling," semantic gloss of samādhi). |

## 3. चित्त — citta

**"That which has been perceived"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Root** | √cit — "to perceive, be conscious of, think" |
| **Prefixes** | (none) |
| **Suffix** | -ta (past passive participle, substantivized) |
| **Segmentation** | √cit + -ta |
| **Confidence** | High |

### What It Means

Citta is the central psychological term of the Yoga Sutras. It appears in the system's defining statement — YS 1.2: "Yoga is the cessation of the fluctuations of citta" (yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ).

Citta is not identical to the Western concept of "mind." In the Sāṃkhya-Yoga framework, citta is the entire apparatus of inner experience: intellect (buddhi), ego-sense (ahaṃkāra), and thinking faculty (manas). Crucially, citta belongs to prakṛti (material nature), not to puruṣa (pure consciousness). Stilling citta does not destroy awareness — it reveals the awareness that was always present beneath the fluctuations.

In Buddhist Abhidharma, citta refers to momentary units of consciousness — a significantly different usage.

### Key Sutra

- **YS 1.2**: yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ — "Yoga is the cessation of the turnings of the mind-field"

### Chinese Equivalent (Provisional)

| Chinese | Pinyin | Type | Notes |
|---------|--------|------|-------|
| 心 | xīn | Semantic | "Heart-mind." Broader than citta — includes emotional/volitional dimensions. Provisional mapping. |

## 4. निरोध — nirodha

**"A complete holding-back"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Root** | √rudh — "to obstruct, stop, restrain" |
| **Prefix** | ni- ("down, into" — here intensifying: complete restraint) |
| **Suffix** | -a (action noun) |
| **Segmentation** | ni + √rudh + -a |
| **Confidence** | High |

### What It Means

Nirodha is the operative word in the Yoga Sutras' definition of yoga (YS 1.2). It names what yoga *does*: it brings about the cessation of citta's fluctuations. Nirodha is not annihilation — it is a quieting, a settling of the mind's habitual activity so that pure awareness (puruṣa) can rest in its own nature.

In Buddhism, nirodha is the third Noble Truth — the cessation of suffering (duḥkha). The word is shared across traditions, but the metaphysics differ: in Yoga, cessation reveals a permanent self (puruṣa); in Buddhism, cessation reveals that no permanent self was ever there.

### A Note on "Suppression" vs. "Cessation"

The root √rudh means "to obstruct," which might suggest forceful suppression. Most modern scholars read Patañjali's nirodha as "cessation" or "stilling" — a progressive calming achieved through practice and non-attachment (YS 1.12), not a violent shutting-down of the mind. This is an interpretive question, not a morphological one.

### Key Sutras

- **YS 1.2**: yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ
- **YS 1.12**: abhyāsa-vairāgyābhyāṃ tan-nirodhaḥ — "That cessation [comes] through practice and non-attachment"
- **YS 3.9**: nirodha-pariṇāma — "the transformation toward cessation"

### Chinese Equivalent (Provisional)

| Chinese | Pinyin | Type | Notes |
|---------|--------|------|-------|
| 滅 | miè | Semantic | "Extinction, cessation." Standard in the Four Noble Truths (苦集滅道). Applies to the Buddhist usage; Yoga Sutras-specific mapping deferred. |

## 5. प्रज्ञा — prajñā

**"Higher knowing"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Root** | √jñā — "to know, perceive, understand" |
| **Prefix** | pra- ("forth, forward" — here: higher, superior) |
| **Suffixes** | (none — formed directly from the prefixed root as a feminine noun) |
| **Segmentation** | pra + √jñā |
| **Confidence** | High |

### What It Means

Prajñā is the direct, non-inferential wisdom that arises in samādhi. Patañjali calls it "truth-bearing" (ṛtaṃbharā, YS 1.48) and explicitly distinguishes it from knowledge gained by scripture or reasoning — it is experiential insight that carries truth directly, without the mediation of concepts.

In Buddhism, prajñā (Pali: paññā) is one of the three core trainings alongside ethical conduct (śīla) and concentration (samādhi). The Prajñāpāramitā ("Perfection of Wisdom") literature, including the Heart Sutra, places prajñā at the center of the Buddhist path — as the wisdom that perceives emptiness (śūnyatā).

Both traditions treat prajñā as the highest form of knowing. But what it knows differs: in Yoga, it knows the distinction between puruṣa and prakṛti. In Buddhism, it knows emptiness.

### Key Sutras

- **YS 1.48**: ṛtaṃbharā tatra prajñā — "The wisdom there is truth-bearing"
- **YS 1.49**: This wisdom "has a different domain from scripture or inference"

### Chinese Equivalents

| Chinese | Pinyin | Type | Notes |
|---------|--------|------|-------|
| 般若 | bōrě | Phonetic | From Prakrit paññā. Deliberately left untranslated to preserve doctrinal weight. One of the most important loanwords in Chinese Buddhism. |
| 慧 | huì | Semantic | "Wisdom." Used in the three trainings formula: 戒定慧 (śīla, samādhi, prajñā). |

### Why 般若 Was Not Translated

Chinese translators *could* have rendered prajñā as 慧 (huì, "wisdom") everywhere — and sometimes did. But for the Prajñāpāramitā literature and doctrinal discussions, they preserved the phonetic 般若 precisely *because* they judged the concept to be irreducibly foreign. Using a Chinese word like "wisdom" would have implied a false equivalence with existing Chinese philosophical concepts. The loanword signaled: this requires new understanding.

## 6. वृत्ति — vṛtti

**"A turning"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Root** | √vṛt — "to turn, revolve, move" |
| **Prefixes** | (none) |
| **Suffix** | -ti (action noun) |
| **Segmentation** | √vṛt + -ti |
| **Confidence** | High |

### What It Means

Vṛtti appears in the defining sutra of the Yoga Sutras (YS 1.2: yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ) and names what yoga acts upon — the turnings or fluctuations of the mind-field. From the root √vṛt ("to turn"), vṛtti is literally "a turning" — the ongoing revolutions of mental activity.

Patañjali classifies five types of vṛtti (YS 1.5-1.11): valid cognition (pramāṇa), error (viparyaya), conceptual construction (vikalpa), sleep (nidrā), and memory (smṛti). A crucial point for the professor: vṛttis are classified as painful or non-painful, but *all* vṛttis — even correct, pleasant ones — must be stilled for yoga to occur. The goal is not to replace bad thoughts with good ones but to quiet the entire turning mechanism.

### Why Translation Matters Here

This is one of the clearest cases in the project where the English translation carries more interpretive weight than the Sanskrit. The root √vṛt simply means "to turn." But translators have rendered vṛtti as:

- **"fluctuation"** (Feuerstein) — implies oscillation
- **"modification"** (Taimni) — implies neutral change
- **"whirlpool"** (Satchidananda) — implies violent churning
- **"turning"** (literal) — the most neutral choice

Each creates a different image of what the mind does. The Sanskrit itself does not privilege any of these connotations.

### Chinese Equivalent

None. Vṛtti is a Yoga Sutras technical term without a standard Chinese Buddhist equivalent. The Yoga Sutras were not systematically translated into Chinese.

## 7. विवेकख्याति — viveka-khyāti

**"The vision of discrimination"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Roots** | √vic — "to sift, separate" + √khyā — "to be known, appear" |
| **Prefix** | vi- ("apart, asunder") |
| **Suffixes** | -a (action noun for viveka) + -ti (action noun for khyāti) |
| **Compound** | Tatpuruṣa (genitive: "the vision *of* discrimination") |
| **Segmentation** | vi + √vic + -a \| √khyā + -ti |
| **Confidence** | High |

### What It Means

Viveka-khyāti is the culminating insight of the Yoga Sutras. It is the direct, sustained perception that pure awareness (puruṣa) and material nature (prakṛti, which includes the mind) are fundamentally distinct. This is not an intellectual conclusion but a perceptual clarity — you *see* the difference, as directly as you see color.

Patañjali says that when this discernment becomes unbroken (aviplavā), it is the means of removing ignorance (YS 2.26). The entire practice of yoga — all eight limbs — exists to produce this vision.

### Why It Matters for Cross-Cultural Comparison

Viveka-khyāti marks one of the sharpest divergences between Yoga and Buddhism. Buddhist insight practice (vipaśyanā) also aims at a transformative seeing — but what it sees is emptiness and non-self. Viveka-khyāti sees the *eternal distinctness of a real self*. Same method (sustained meditative perception), opposite metaphysical conclusion.

### Chinese Equivalent

None. This is a Sāṃkhya-Yoga technical compound without a standard Chinese rendering. Confirmed in GRETIL's Sāṃkhyatattvavivecana and Brahmasūtra sub-commentary, establishing its use across Indian philosophical literature.

## 8. क्लेश — kleśa

**"That which afflicts"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Root** | √kliś — "to torment, afflict, cause pain" |
| **Prefixes** | (none) |
| **Suffix** | -a (result noun) |
| **Segmentation** | √kliś + -a |
| **Confidence** | High |

### What It Means

The kleśas are Patañjali's diagnosis of why beings suffer. There are exactly five (YS 2.3): ignorance (avidyā), ego-sense (asmitā), attachment (rāga), aversion (dveṣa), and clinging to life (abhiniveśa). These are not passing emotions or moral failings — they are deep structural distortions in how the mind relates to experience. Avidyā is the root from which the other four grow (YS 2.4).

A key detail for understanding practice: kleśas exist in four states — dormant, attenuated, alternating, or fully active. The goal is to move them from active to attenuated to dormant, through sustained practice and discriminative insight.

### Buddhist Parallel

Buddhism also uses kleśa (Pali: kilesa) for mental afflictions, but enumerates them differently — typically three root poisons (rāga, dveṣa, moha) rather than Patañjali's five. The shared term suggests historical contact between the traditions.

### Chinese Equivalent

| Chinese | Pinyin | Type | Notes |
|---------|--------|------|-------|
| 煩惱 | fánnǎo | Semantic | "Vexation-affliction." One of the most stable semantic mappings in Chinese Buddhist translation. Ubiquitous across the canon. |

## 9. अविद्या — avidyā

**"Non-knowledge"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Root** | √vid — "to know, find, understand" |
| **Prefix** | a- ("not, without") |
| **Suffix** | -yā (feminine abstract noun) |
| **Segmentation** | a + √vid + -yā |
| **Confidence** | High |

### What It Means

Avidyā is not mere ignorance — not simply lacking information. It is a positive misperception: actively seeing things as they are not. Patañjali defines it with clinical precision (YS 2.5): avidyā is taking the impermanent for permanent, the impure for pure, suffering for pleasure, and the non-self for the self.

This is the root kleśa. All other afflictions grow from this soil. The entire project of yoga exists to uproot it.

### Three Traditions, Same Word, Different Claims

Avidyā appears in Yoga, Vedānta, and Buddhism, but what it conceals differs:

| Tradition | What avidyā conceals | What removes it |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------------|
| **Yoga (Patañjali)** | The distinction between puruṣa and prakṛti | Viveka-khyāti (discriminative discernment) |
| **Advaita Vedānta (Śaṅkara)** | The non-dual Brahman | Ātma-jñāna (self-knowledge) |
| **Buddhism** | The Four Noble Truths / dependent origination | Prajñā / vipaśyanā (insight into emptiness) |

This is one of the most important terms for cross-cultural analysis: the same diagnosis ("the root problem is a failure of seeing"), the same prescription ("see clearly"), but radically different metaphysics.

### Chinese Equivalent

| Chinese | Pinyin | Type | Notes |
|---------|--------|------|-------|
| 無明 | wúmíng | Semantic | "Without brightness." A structural calque: 無 (without) + 明 (clarity) mirrors a- (without) + vidyā (knowledge). First link of dependent origination in Chinese Buddhist texts. Confirmed in T01n0001, T01n0003. |

The CBETA search confirmed the canonical formula: 若無明滅盡是時則無行 ("when avidyā ceases, then saṃskāra ceases") in the Dīrghāgama.

## 10. संस्कार — saṃskāra

**"A thorough making"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Root** | √kṛ — "to do, make, perform" |
| **Prefix** | sam- ("together, completely, well") |
| **Suffix** | -a (with guṇa of root: kṛ → kāra) |
| **Segmentation** | sam + √kṛ + -a |
| **Confidence** | High |

### What It Means

Saṃskāra may be the most semantically rich word in the entire seed list. In different contexts it means:

- **Ritual**: consecration, sacrament (the saṃskāras of life: birth, marriage, death)
- **Grammar**: refinement (saṃskṛta = "refined" = the name of the Sanskrit language itself)
- **Yoga Sutras**: latent impressions — subliminal traces left in the mind by every experience

In the Yoga Sutras, saṃskāras are the residue of past actions and experiences, stored in citta. They shape how we perceive, desire, and act without our being aware of them — a concept strikingly close to the modern notion of unconscious conditioning. Crucially, saṃskāras from samādhi can overwrite saṃskāras from ordinary experience (YS 1.50). This is the mechanism by which practice transforms the mind: not by destroying old patterns directly, but by laying down new, deeper ones.

### Buddhist Parallel

Buddhist saṅkhāra (Pali) is the second link in dependent origination (avidyā conditions saṅkhāra) and one of the five aggregates (skandhas). In the Buddhist context, the emphasis falls on "volitional formations" — active constructive mental activity — rather than latent traces. The Yogic and Buddhist meanings overlap but are not identical.

### Chinese Equivalent

| Chinese | Pinyin | Type | Notes |
|---------|--------|------|-------|
| 行 | xíng | Semantic | "Action, movement, formation." Renders Buddhist saṅkhāra in the five aggregates (五蘊) and dependent origination (十二因緣). Does not capture the Yogic sense of "latent subliminal trace." |

The character 行 foregrounds active formation rather than passive residue — a case where the Chinese translation shifts the concept's center of gravity.

## 11. कैवल्य — kaivalya

**"Aloneness"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Base** | kevala (adjective) — "alone, exclusive, absolute" |
| **Suffix** | -ya (abstract noun: "the state of being kevala") |
| **Segmentation** | kevala + -ya |
| **Confidence** | High |

### What It Means

Kaivalya is the goal — the final liberation described in the Yoga Sutras. It is derived not from a verbal root but from the adjective kevala ("alone"), making it literally "aloneness" or "the state of being exclusively oneself."

This is not loneliness. In Sāṃkhya-Yoga metaphysics, kaivalya is the condition in which puruṣa (pure awareness) stands free in its own nature, permanently disentangled from prakṛti (material nature). The guṇas return to their latent state. Awareness is aware of itself alone — which is, in this system, the highest possible state.

### Why "Aloneness" Is Not "Union"

Popular usage treats "yoga" as meaning "union." But the goal of Patañjali's system is the opposite: separation. Kaivalya is the isolation of pure awareness from everything that is not itself. This is a fundamental point that surprises many students and is worth emphasizing for the professor.

### How It Differs from Buddhist and Vedāntic Liberation

| System | Goal | What happens |
|--------|------|-------------|
| **Yoga (Patañjali)** | Kaivalya | Puruṣa isolated from prakṛti; awareness alone |
| **Advaita Vedānta** | Mokṣa | Self (ātman) recognized as identical with Brahman; non-dual union |
| **Buddhism** | Nirvāṇa (涅槃) | Cessation of craving; end of rebirth cycle; no permanent self posited |

### Chinese Equivalent

None. Kaivalya is a Sāṃkhya-Yoga technical term. Buddhist liberation is nirvāṇa (涅槃, nièpán), which carries very different metaphysical commitments.

## 12. अभ्यास — abhyāsa

**"A repeated casting-toward"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Root** | √as — "to throw, cast" |
| **Prefixes** | abhi- ("toward, upon") + ā- ("unto") |
| **Suffix** | -a (action noun) |
| **Segmentation** | abhi + ā + √as + -a |
| **Confidence** | Medium |

### What It Means

Abhyāsa is one of the two means to nirodha, paired with vairāgya (non-attachment) in YS 1.12. Patañjali defines it as "the effort toward steadiness" (sthitau yatnaḥ, YS 1.13) — the sustained, intentional return to practice.

Three conditions make abhyāsa firm (YS 1.14):
1. **Long duration** (dīrgha-kāla) — not days but years
2. **Unbroken continuity** (nairantarya) — without gaps
3. **Reverent dedication** (satkāra) — not mechanical repetition but devoted effort

This manages expectations: the text is saying explicitly that transformation is slow, requires consistency, and demands care. For a meditation-focused class, this is one of the most practically relevant sutras.

### Why Confidence Is Medium

The morphology is the only genuinely ambiguous case in this batch. The standard derivation is abhi + ā + √as (4th class, "to throw, cast"), yielding "a repeated throwing-toward." But an alternative derivation from √as ("to sit") — yielding something closer to "sitting near" (cf. upāsana) — has been proposed. Monier-Williams and Apte both give √as ("to throw") as primary, and the Dhvanyāloka commentary glosses abhyāsa as "paunaḥpunya" ("repetition"), supporting the iterative sense. We follow the mainstream derivation but flag the ambiguity.

### Chinese Equivalent

None. Abhyāsa is a Yoga Sutras technical term. The concept of sustained practice exists across Buddhist traditions but is not typically rendered by a single term corresponding to abhyāsa.

## 13. वैराग्य — vairāgya

**"Freedom from coloring"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Root** | √rañj — "to be colored, dyed, excited, attached" |
| **Prefix** | vi- ("without") |
| **Suffix** | -ya (abstract noun via vṛddhi: virāga → vairāgya) |
| **Segmentation** | vi + rāga + -ya |
| **Confidence** | High |

### What It Means

Vairāgya is the counterpart to abhyāsa — the two together are the means to nirodha (YS 1.12). If abhyāsa is the active effort of practice, vairāgya is the corresponding release. The etymological image is vivid: rāga means "coloring" or "dyeing" — the way desire tinges or stains awareness. Vairāgya is the removal of that coloring, leaving awareness clear and unstained.

Patañjali describes two levels. Ordinary vairāgya (YS 1.15) is freedom from craving for sensory objects. The higher vairāgya (YS 1.16) is freedom from craving for the guṇas themselves — for *any* experience, however subtle. This higher form arises only from direct knowledge of puruṣa.

### Why the Pairing Matters

The abhyāsa-vairāgya pair is one of the Yoga Sutras' most psychologically astute insights. Effort without non-attachment becomes strain and grasping — even meditation can become something you cling to. Non-attachment without effort becomes passivity and withdrawal. Patañjali insists on both together, in balance.

The Aṣṭāvakragītā opens with the question "how is vairāgya attained?" (SARIT 1.1) — establishing it as a central concern well beyond the Yoga Sutras.

### Chinese Equivalent

None. Vairāgya is a Yoga Sutras technical term without a standard Chinese Buddhist rendering.

## 14. धारणा — dhāraṇā

**"A holding steady"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Root** | √dhṛ — "to hold, bear, support, maintain" |
| **Suffixes** | -ana (action noun, with guṇa: dhṛ → dhār) + -ā (feminine) |
| **Segmentation** | √dhṛ + -ana + -ā |
| **Confidence** | High |

### What It Means

Dhāraṇā is concentration in the strict sense: "binding citta to a place" (deśa-bandhaḥ, YS 3.1). The "place" can be external (a candle flame, a mantra) or internal (the navel center, the heart, the tip of the nose). What matters is the act of choosing a single focus and holding attention there.

### Where It Sits in Practice

Dhāraṇā is the sixth limb and the first of the three inner limbs (antaraṅga). This matters for understanding the practice sequence:

| Limb | Name | What happens |
|------|------|-------------|
| 6 | **Dhāraṇā** | Fix attention on a single point |
| 7 | **Dhyāna** | That attention becomes an unbroken stream |
| 8 | **Samādhi** | The observer dissolves into the object |

These are not three separate practices but three stages of a single deepening. For a meditator, dhāraṇā is where "meditation" in the recognizable sense begins — the moment of choosing an anchor and returning to it.

### A Cognate Worth Noting

The root √dhṛ also gives us *dharma* (that which upholds). Dhāraṇā and dharma are etymological siblings — both involve the fundamental act of "holding."

### Chinese Equivalent

None as a standalone term. Dhāraṇā is part of the Yoga Sutras' technical vocabulary for the inner limbs.

## 15. संयम — saṃyama

**"A complete holding-together"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Root** | √yam — "to restrain, hold in, control" |
| **Prefix** | sam- ("together, completely") |
| **Suffix** | -a (action noun) |
| **Segmentation** | sam + √yam + -a |
| **Confidence** | High |

### What It Means

Saṃyama is Patañjali's name for dhāraṇā, dhyāna, and samādhi operating as a unified process. YS 3.4: "the three together on a single object are saṃyama." It is not a fourth practice added to the other three but their integration — the name for what happens when the entire inner sequence functions as one.

### Why It Matters: The Engine of the Vibhūti Pāda

Nearly all of YS chapter 3 (the Vibhūti Pāda) follows a single pattern: "saṃyama on X yields knowledge of Y." Examples:

- Saṃyama on the three transformations → knowledge of past and future
- Saṃyama on the distinction between word and meaning → understanding of all beings' speech
- Saṃyama on the sun → knowledge of the cosmic regions

Saṃyama is thus the mechanism by which meditative absorption produces specific insights and powers (siddhis). Understanding this term unlocks the entire structure of chapter 3.

### Root Connections

The root √yam gives the Yoga Sutras three of its key terms: yama (ethical restraints, limb 1), niyama (observances, limb 2), and saṃyama (integrated absorption). All share the notion of "holding" or "restraining," but at increasingly refined levels — from holding behavior, to holding habits, to holding awareness itself.

### Chinese Equivalent

None for the Yogic technical sense. The Jātakamālā (GRETIL) uses saṃyama in the more general Buddhist sense of "self-restraint."

## 16. प्रत्याहार — pratyāhāra

**"A drawing-back-toward"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Root** | √hṛ — "to take, seize, carry" |
| **Prefixes** | prati- ("back") + ā- ("toward oneself") |
| **Suffix** | -a (action noun) |
| **Segmentation** | prati + ā + √hṛ + -a |
| **Confidence** | High |

### What It Means

Pratyāhāra is the fifth limb — the hinge between the outer and inner practices. It describes the process by which the senses disengage from their objects and follow the mind inward. Patañjali's image (YS 2.54) is that the senses "imitate the own-form of citta" — just as bees follow the queen, the senses follow the mind when it withdraws.

This is *not* forceful suppression of sensation. It is a natural turning-inward: when the mind withdraws from external engagement, the senses follow. The result is "supreme mastery over the senses" (paramā vaśyatā, YS 2.55).

### The Hinge of the System

| External limbs (1-4) | | Internal limbs (6-8) |
|---|---|---|
| Yama, niyama, āsana, prāṇāyāma | **→ Pratyāhāra (5) →** | Dhāraṇā, dhyāna, samādhi |

Everything before pratyāhāra prepares the body and behavior. Everything after it works directly with attention. For a meditator, pratyāhāra is the transition from "settling in" to "turning inward."

### A Grammatical Homonym

In Pāṇini's Sanskrit grammar, pratyāhāra means an abbreviatory formula — a way of "gathering together" phonemes into a shorthand notation. Same etymology (prati + ā + √hṛ = "drawing together"), completely different domain. This is a useful example for the professor of how Sanskrit technical vocabulary works: the same morphological logic applied to different subject matter.

### Chinese Equivalent

None. Pratyāhāra is a Yoga Sutras technical term.

## 17. पुरुष — puruṣa

**"The person" (etymology debated)**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Root** | Uncertain — see below |
| **Segmentation** | Debated |
| **Confidence** | Medium (morphology); High (philosophical analysis) |

### What It Means

Puruṣa is the metaphysical foundation of the entire Yoga Sutras: pure, unchanging consciousness, radically distinct from prakṛti (material nature). Puruṣa does not act, think, or change. It witnesses. Everything else — the mind, the body, the senses, all experience — belongs to prakṛti. The root error (avidyā) is confusing puruṣa with prakṛti. Liberation (kaivalya) is their permanent separation.

### Why the Etymology Is Genuinely Uncertain

This is the only term in the entire seed list where honest analysis must say: the derivation is not settled. Major proposals:

1. **puri-śaya** — "one who lies in the city [of the body]." Found in the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad. Widely cited but a folk etymology, not a standard linguistic derivation.
2. **puru + nominal suffix** — from puru ("much, full"), yielding "the full one" or "the abundant one."
3. **Connection to √pṝ** ("to fill") — via the old form pūru.
4. **Pre-transparent** — a very old word whose original formation is lost. Mayrhofer's Etymological Dictionary treats it as uncertain.

For this project, we prioritize the philosophical analysis (which is solid) over the etymological one (which is not).

### The Major Metaphysical Divergence

| System | Position on puruṣa/self |
|--------|------------------------|
| **Sāṃkhya-Yoga** | Real, permanent, plural (each being has its own puruṣa) |
| **Advaita Vedānta** | Real, permanent, singular (ātman = Brahman) |
| **Buddhism** | Denied (anātman — no permanent self exists) |

This is the deepest philosophical fault line in Indian thought, and puruṣa is its focus.

### Chinese Equivalent

None — because the concept is rejected by Buddhism. Chinese Buddhist texts sometimes mention 神我 (shénwǒ, "spirit-self") when *criticizing* the Sāṃkhya position, but this is polemical, not a translation equivalent.

## 18. स्वरूप — svarūpa

**"Own form"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Components** | sva ("own") + rūpa ("form, appearance, nature") |
| **Compound type** | Karmadhāraya (descriptive: "own form") |
| **Segmentation** | sva + rūpa |
| **Confidence** | High |

### What It Means

Svarūpa appears in what may be the most important pair of sutras in the entire text:

> **YS 1.2**: yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ — "Yoga is the cessation of the turnings of citta"
> **YS 1.3**: tadā draṣṭuḥ svarūpe 'vasthānam — "Then the seer abides in its own form"

Together, these two sutras state: when the mind stills, awareness recognizes its own nature. Svarūpa names what is revealed when you stop being your thoughts — the "own form" of puruṣa, which was always present but obscured.

The term also appears in the definition of samādhi (YS 3.3: "svarūpa-śūnyam iva" — "as if empty of its own form"), where it describes the dissolution of the observer's separate identity during absorption.

### The Deepest Philosophical Contrast in the Project

In the Yoga Sutras, everything *has* a svarūpa — an essential own-nature. Puruṣa's svarūpa is pure awareness. The goal is to rest in it.

In Mādhyamaka Buddhism, nothing *has* svarūpa (svabhāva). The central insight is svabhāva-śūnyatā — "emptiness of own-nature." The goal is to *see through* the illusion of inherent nature.

Same word. Opposite claims. This is one of the sharpest contrasts available for cross-cultural analysis.

### Chinese Equivalent (Provisional)

| Chinese | Pinyin | Type | Notes |
|---------|--------|------|-------|
| 自性 | zìxìng | Semantic | "Own-nature." Corresponds more precisely to svabhāva than to svarūpa, but the terms overlap. In Chinese Buddhism, 自性 appears mainly in debates about whether things have inherent nature (自性空 = "empty of own-nature"). The Chan/Zen expression 見性 (jiànxìng, "seeing one's nature") superficially resembles svarūpe 'vasthānam but the metaphysics differ completely. Provisional mapping. |

## 19. अभिनिवेश — abhiniveśa

**"A settling-deeply-into"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Root** | √viś — "to enter, settle into, take possession of" |
| **Prefixes** | abhi- ("toward, upon") + ni- ("down, into") |
| **Suffix** | -a (result noun) |
| **Segmentation** | abhi + ni + √viś + -a |
| **Confidence** | High |

### What It Means

Abhiniveśa is the fifth kleśa — usually translated as "clinging to life" or "fear of death." But the Sanskrit is subtler: it is the instinctive, pre-rational will to continue existing. Three prefixes (abhi + ni + √viś) pile up to create a sense of *deep settling-into* — awareness that has so thoroughly entered the survival drive that it cannot easily extract itself.

### Patañjali's Remarkable Observation

YS 2.9 contains one of the Yoga Sutras' most psychologically striking claims: abhiniveśa "flows by its own momentum" (sva-rasa-vāhī) and is "firmly established even in the wise" (viduṣo 'pi tathārūḍhaḥ). This means:

- It is not a product of reasoning. You cannot think your way out of it.
- It is not a product of experience in this life. Even newborns show it.
- Even someone who intellectually understands that death is not to be feared still instinctively recoils from it.

Patañjali's explanation is that abhiniveśa is rooted in saṃskāras from past deaths — the accumulated memory of having died before generates an automatic survival drive. This is one of the text's most original ideas.

### Why It Matters for Practice

Abhiniveśa reveals that the kleśas are not merely intellectual errors. You cannot simply "know better" and be free. The deepest afflictions are embodied and instinctual, which is why sustained practice (abhyāsa) is necessary — intellectual insight alone is not enough. For a meditation-focused class, abhiniveśa names the deepest resistance a practitioner encounters: the organism's refusal to let go.

### Chinese Equivalent

None. Abhiniveśa is a Yoga Sutras technical term. Buddhist treatments of attachment to existence (bhavataṇhā in Pali) are conceptually related but terminologically distinct.

## 20. असम्प्रज्ञात — asamprajñāta

**"Not-fully-cognized"**

| | |
|-|-|
| **Root** | √jñā — "to know, perceive, understand" |
| **Prefixes** | a- ("not") + sam- ("completely") + pra- ("forth, higher") |
| **Suffix** | -ta (past passive participle) |
| **Segmentation** | a + sam + pra + √jñā + -ta |
| **Confidence** | High (morphology); Medium (doctrinal scope) |

### What It Means

Asamprajñāta-samādhi is the higher of the two broad types of samādhi. In samprajñāta-samādhi (YS 1.17), awareness is absorbed but still has cognitive content — there is still *something being known*: deliberation (vitarka), reflection (vicāra), bliss (ānanda), or the bare sense of "I am" (asmitā). In asamprajñāta, even that drops away. What remains is awareness without an object, with only a residue of saṃskāras (YS 1.18).

### A Critical Textual Detail

The word "asamprajñāta" does not actually appear in the Yoga Sutras themselves. YS 1.18 says only "anyaḥ" — "the other [samādhi]." The label "asamprajñāta" comes from Vyāsa's Bhāṣya, the earliest extant commentary, which has been treated as virtually canonical. This is worth noting because it means the traditional classification (samprajñāta vs. asamprajñāta) is a commentarial framework, not Patañjali's own explicit terminology.

### The Scholarly Debate

There is genuine disagreement about what asamprajñāta-samādhi is:

| Reading | Scholar(s) | Claim |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| **Stable state** | Vyāsa (traditional) | A distinct, lasting state beyond all cognitive samādhi |
| **Transitional moment** | Whicher, some modern scholars | The gap between one level of samprajñāta and the next |
| **Identical with nirbīja** | Some commentators | Same as "seedless samādhi" (YS 1.51) |

The Yoga Sutras themselves are too terse to resolve this definitively, which is precisely why the commentary tradition became so important.

### Chinese Equivalent

None. This is a Yoga Sutras technical term. The concept of "formless absorption" in Buddhist meditation (arūpa-jhāna) is structurally analogous but doctrinally distinct.

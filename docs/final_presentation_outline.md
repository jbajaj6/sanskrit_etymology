# Final Presentation Outline

**Title**: What Sanskrit Words Know: Morphology, Meditation, and the Journey from India to China

**Duration**: 8–10 minutes
**Audience**: Philosophy class with meditation emphasis, non-technical

---

## Section 1: Opening Motivation (1 min)

**Slide**: YS 1.2 in Devanagari + transliteration + translation

- Open with the most famous sutra: yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ
- Point out that this single sentence contains three technical terms (citta, vṛtti, nirodha), each built from specific roots and prefixes
- Claim: the *structure* of these words is not linguistic trivia — it is a compressed philosophical argument

## Section 2: What This Project Studies (1 min)

- 30 Sanskrit terms from the Yoga Sutras and Buddhist meditation traditions
- 20 fully analyzed for morphological structure and philosophical meaning
- 9 traced into Chinese Buddhist vocabulary
- Method: break each word into root + prefix + suffix, then compare the literal meaning with the philosophical meaning

## Section 3: How Sanskrit Word-Building Works (1.5 min)

**Case study: samādhi**

- Root: √dhā ("to place")
- Prefixes: sam- ("together") + ā- ("toward")
- Suffix: -i (action noun)
- Literal: "a complete placing-together"
- Philosophical: meditative absorption where knower, knowing, and known merge
- Sidebar: correct the folk etymology (sama + ādhi ≠ "equal placing")

**Key point**: The word's architecture *argues* for its meaning. "Placing together" is not a metaphor added later — it is built into the word.

## Section 4: Sanskrit Case Studies (3 min)

### 4a. nirodha — "A complete holding-back"

- ni + √rudh + -a
- Root √rudh means "to restrain" — not "to destroy"
- Why this matters: "cessation" vs. "suppression" vs. "stilling" are philosophically different translations
- The Chinese 滅 ("extinction") overshoots: it implies annihilation, while nirodha implies quieting

### 4b. abhiniveśa — "A deep settling-into"

- abhi + ni + √viś + -a (triple prefix!)
- The morphology physically enacts the concept: layer after layer of settling deeper
- "Flows by its own momentum, even in the wise" (YS 2.9)
- This is not an intellectual error — it is an embodied survival drive
- Most original psychological observation in the Yoga Sutras

### 4c. saṃskāra — "A thorough making"

- sam + √kṛ + -a
- Three lives of one word: ritual consecration, latent impression, volitional formation
- The name "Sanskrit" (saṃskṛta, "refined") comes from the same root
- In the Yoga Sutras: the unconscious residue of experience that shapes future perception
- Crucial for understanding why insight alone isn't enough — old patterns must be overwritten

## Section 5: India-to-China Mapping (2 min)

### 5a. dhyāna → 禪 → chán → zen

- The strongest transmission case
- Phonetic chain: dhyāna → jhāna (Prakrit) → chánnà (禪那) → chán (禪) → zen
- An entire school of Buddhism named after this one word
- Four Chinese renderings: transliteration, abbreviation, hybrid, semantic
- Xuanzang's 靜慮 ("quiet contemplation") — a 7th-century attempt at precision

### 5b. avidyā → 無明 — "Without brightness"

- A morphological mirror: 無 (without) + 明 (brightness) = a- (not) + vidyā (knowledge)
- But the translators made a choice: "brightness" instead of "knowledge"
- Small shift: the Chinese image is phenomenological (darkness), the Sanskrit is epistemological (not-knowing)
- Shows that even the most faithful translations are acts of interpretation

### 5c. svarūpa → 自性 — The cautionary case (brief)

- In Yoga: "own form" — what puruṣa *is*; meditation reveals it
- In Chinese Mādhyamaka: 自性 — what nothing *has*; meditation sees through it
- Same word, opposite claims
- The gap is the finding

## Section 6: What the Project Reveals (1 min)

Three takeaways:

1. **Sanskrit word-structure carries philosophical weight.** Roots and prefixes are not decoration — they compress arguments about what concepts mean.
2. **Translation is always interpretation.** Every Chinese rendering chose an emphasis: sound or meaning, felt experience or structural cause, darkness or ignorance.
3. **Cross-cultural transmission is partial.** The strongest connections are real (dhyāna → zen). But the Yoga Sutras' specialized vocabulary has no Chinese counterpart — these are different traditions sharing a vocabulary, not a single tradition in two languages.

## Section 8: Limits and Caution (30 sec)

- The Yoga Sutras were never systematically translated into Chinese
- Chinese Buddhist renderings reflect Buddhist usage, not Yogic usage
- Phonetic similarity is not proof of transmission
- This project maps terms, not entire philosophical systems

## Section 9: Conclusion (30 sec)

Return to YS 1.2. Now the audience can hear each word differently:

- **yogaś** — "yoking" (but Patañjali's goal is separation, not union)
- **citta** — not "mind" but "the perceived" — the accumulated field of awareness
- **vṛtti** — not "thoughts" but "turnings" — the ongoing revolution of mental activity
- **nirodhaḥ** — not "stopping" but "holding back" — a quieting, not a destruction

The words knew what they meant all along. We just had to learn how to listen.

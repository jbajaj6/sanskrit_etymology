# What Sanskrit Words Know: Morphology, Meditation, and the Journey from India to China

## Introduction

The Yoga Sutras of Patañjali are among the most concise texts in the Indian philosophical tradition. In approximately 196 aphorisms, Patañjali lays out a complete theory of mind, a diagnosis of suffering, and a systematic path to liberation. The text's brevity means that individual words carry extraordinary weight — each term is a compressed argument, and understanding the text requires understanding its vocabulary at the deepest available level.

This project takes that requirement seriously. It analyzes 20 key Sanskrit terms from the Yoga Sutras and related meditation traditions at the level of morphological structure: roots, prefixes, suffixes, and compound types. The goal is to show that the internal architecture of Sanskrit words is not merely a linguistic curiosity but a window into philosophical meaning — that the way a word is *built* reveals something about what it *claims*.

The project then extends this analysis cross-culturally. When Buddhist concepts traveled from India to China beginning in the first centuries CE, Chinese translators faced the challenge of rendering Sanskrit philosophical vocabulary in a language with entirely different structure and intellectual traditions. For 9 of the 20 analyzed terms, this project traces the Chinese renderings and evaluates what was preserved, what changed, and what was lost. The result is a picture of both semantic continuity and doctrinal shift — a record of how meaning survives, and how it mutates, in cross-cultural transmission.

## Method

### Sanskrit Morphological Analysis

Each term was analyzed by identifying its verbal root (dhātu), prefixes (upasarga), suffixes (pratyaya), and compound type (if applicable). The literal meaning was then reconstructed from these components and compared with the term's technical philosophical meaning in the Yoga Sutras.

Root meanings and derivations follow standard Sanskrit reference works, primarily the Monier-Williams *Sanskrit-English Dictionary* and Apte's *Practical Sanskrit-English Dictionary*. Where a derivation is debated among scholars, the major positions are noted and a confidence rating (high, medium, or low) is assigned to the morphological analysis. The project does not fabricate etymologies or present uncertain derivations as settled.

### Cross-Cultural Mapping

For terms that entered Chinese through Buddhist translation, the project evaluates attested Chinese counterparts using the CBETA digital corpus of Chinese Buddhist texts (cited by Taishō catalogue number). Each mapping is classified by type: transliteration (sound-based), semantic translation (meaning-based), hybrid, or broad equivalent. Phonetic evidence is evaluated against established historical linguistic reconstructions (Middle Chinese readings). Semantic evidence is assessed for accuracy of meaning-transfer. Where the Chinese rendering introduces a shift in connotation or philosophical framework, this is documented as a "doctrinal shift."

A key methodological constraint: this project distinguishes carefully between evidence and interpretation. That a Chinese term appears in a specific text is evidence. That a metaphor shift changes philosophical meaning is interpretation. Both are presented, but they are not conflated.

## Findings: Sanskrit Term Analysis

### The Architecture of Meaning

The central finding of the morphological analysis is that Sanskrit philosophical terms are not arbitrary labels. Their internal structure — the specific roots and prefixes chosen — encodes substantive claims about what the concepts mean.

**Samādhi** (समाधि), the culmination of yogic practice, is built from the root √dhā ("to place"), the prefixes sam- ("together, completely") and ā- ("toward"), and the suffix -i (action noun). The literal meaning — "a complete placing-together" — directly describes the phenomenology of meditative absorption: the gathering and settling of dispersed awareness into unified focus. The word does not merely name the state; it describes the process. This also corrects a common folk etymology that derives samādhi from sama ("equal") + ādhi, a derivation not supported by standard Sanskrit grammar.

**Nirodha** (निरोध), the operative word in the Yoga Sutras' definition of yoga (YS 1.2: yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ), is built from ni- (intensifying prefix) + √rudh ("to hold back, restrain") + -a (action noun). The literal meaning — "a complete holding-back" — is significant because different English translations carry different philosophical implications. "Cessation" implies an ending; "suppression" implies force; "stilling" implies a quieting. The root √rudh favors "stilling" or "restraining" over either "ending" or "forcing." This matters for practice: Patañjali's system does not destroy the mind but quiets its habitual activity.

**Abhiniveśa** (अभिनिवेश), the fifth kleśa (affliction), is built from three prefixes stacked over a single root: abhi- ("toward") + ni- ("down into") + √viś ("to enter, settle into") + -a. The triple-prefix structure physically enacts the concept — layers of settling deeper and deeper in. Patañjali describes it as "flowing by its own momentum, firmly established even in the wise" (YS 2.9), meaning that this clinging to existence persists regardless of intellectual understanding. The morphology mirrors the psychology: abhiniveśa is awareness that has *entered into* the survival drive so deeply that it cannot easily extract itself. This is one of the Yoga Sutras' most original contributions — a description of what modern psychology might call a hardwired survival instinct, explained through the mechanism of karmic memory.

**Saṃskāra** (संस्कार), from sam- + √kṛ + -a ("a thorough making"), is the most polysemous term in the project. In ritual contexts, it means "consecration"; in grammar, it means "refinement" (the word saṃskṛta, "Sanskrit," literally means "well-made"); in the Yoga Sutras, it means "latent impression" — the subliminal trace left in citta by every experience. This polysemy is philosophically significant: it shows that the same morphological structure ("thorough making") was applied to radically different domains — ritual, language, and psychology — suggesting a deep conceptual connection between preparation, refinement, and the formation of mental habits.

### What English Translation Obscures

A recurring finding is that English translations compress distinctions that the Sanskrit preserves. The word vṛtti (वृत्ति, from √vṛt, "to turn") simply means "a turning." But translators render it as "fluctuation" (Feuerstein), "modification" (Taimni), "whirlpool" (Satchidananda), or "thought-wave" — each carrying a different image of mental activity that the Sanskrit root does not specify. The root √vṛt is neutral; the English translations are not. This pattern repeats across the vocabulary: each English rendering is an interpretive decision, and awareness of the Sanskrit original reveals which decisions were made.

## Findings: India-to-China Mapping

### The Strongest Case: dhyāna → 禪 → chán → zen

The transmission of dhyāna into Chinese is one of the best-documented loanword paths in Asian languages. The phonetic chain — Sanskrit dhyāna → Pali jhāna (palatalization of the aspirate cluster) → Middle Chinese *dʑian-na (禪那, chánnà) → abbreviated 禪 (chán) → Japanese 禅 (zen) — is attested at every stage.

Chinese translators developed four distinct renderings of this single term: the full transliteration 禪那, the abbreviation 禪, the hybrid compound 禪定 (禪 phonetic + 定 semantic "settling"), and Xuanzang's semantic translation 靜慮 ("quiet contemplation"). The diversity of renderings is itself evidence of sustained engagement with the concept — translators kept returning to this word, trying different strategies, across several centuries.

The doctrinal shift is also significant. In the Yoga Sutras, dhyāna is one specific stage — limb 7 of 8, the unbroken stream of attention preceding absorption. In Chinese Buddhism, 禪 expanded until it named an entire school (Chan) and a total orientation toward awakening that sometimes paradoxically de-emphasized seated meditation. The word traveled; its scope changed.

### Translation as Interpretation: avidyā → 無明

Avidyā ("non-knowledge") was rendered in Chinese as 無明 ("without brightness") — a structural calque that mirrors the Sanskrit morphology almost piece by piece: 無 ("without") = a- ("not"), 明 ("brightness") = vidyā ("knowledge"). But the Chinese translators made a quiet interpretive choice. They selected "brightness" (明) rather than "knowledge" (知) as the base term, foregrounding a metaphor of obscuration rather than epistemological failure. The Sanskrit image is cognitive: a failure of knowing. The Chinese image is perceptual: an absence of light.

This small shift has phenomenological consequences. A practitioner shaped by the Chinese image might experience avidyā as darkness to be dispelled — a clearing of vision. A practitioner shaped by the Sanskrit might experience it as a mistake to be corrected — a realignment of understanding. Both are valid, but they orient practice differently.

### When the Gap Is the Finding: svarūpa → 自性

The weakest mapping in the project is also the most philosophically illuminating. Sanskrit svarūpa ("own form") in the Yoga Sutras denotes what puruṣa *is* — its essential nature, revealed when meditation succeeds (YS 1.3). The closest Chinese candidate is 自性 (zìxìng, "own nature"), but in Chinese Mādhyamaka Buddhism, 自性 is primarily used to *deny* inherent nature: the central Mādhyamaka insight is 自性空 (svabhāva-śūnyatā, "emptiness of own-nature").

The same philosophical question — "does anything have an essential nature?" — receives opposite answers. The Yoga Sutras say yes: puruṣa's svarūpa is real, permanent, and worth discovering. Mādhyamaka says no: the belief that anything has inherent nature is itself the root error. The Chinese term 自性 became the vehicle for the denial of what the Sanskrit term affirms. This is not a translation failure — it is evidence of genuine philosophical divergence, made visible by the vocabulary.

### Three Patterns

Three cross-cutting patterns emerge from the mapping analysis:

1. **Sound-preservation signals doctrinal importance.** When Chinese translators kept the foreign sound — 般若 (bōrě) for prajñā, 三昧 (sānmèi) for samādhi, 禪那 (chánnà) for dhyāna — they were signaling that the concept was too important or too foreign to domesticate. When they translated the meaning instead — 煩惱 (fánnǎo) for kleśa, 無明 (wúmíng) for avidyā — they judged the Chinese rendering sufficient. The translators' choice between sound and meaning is itself evidence of how they weighted these concepts.

2. **All strong mappings are Buddhist, not Yogic.** The Yoga Sutras were never systematically translated into Chinese. Every strong Chinese counterpart in this project comes from Buddhist translation literature. Terms central to the Yoga Sutras but absent from Buddhist usage — vṛtti, viveka-khyāti, kaivalya, abhyāsa — have no Chinese equivalents at all.

3. **The most instructive cases are the failures.** The svarūpa/自性 case demonstrates that mapping failures can be more revealing than mapping successes. The gap between traditions illuminates their deepest commitments.

## Conclusion

This project began with a simple observation: Sanskrit philosophical words are not arbitrary labels but built structures whose architecture carries meaning. Breaking a word like samādhi into sam + ā + √dhā + -i is not a linguistic exercise disconnected from philosophy — it recovers a claim about what meditative absorption *is* that the word's creators embedded in its structure.

The cross-cultural extension revealed something complementary: that meaning changes shape when it crosses linguistic and cultural boundaries, and that the *way* it changes is itself philosophically significant. The Chinese translator who chose "without brightness" over "without knowledge" for avidyā was not making a mistake — they were making an interpretation. The tradition that used 自性 to deny inherent nature while the Yoga Sutras used svarūpa to affirm it was not confused — it was answering the same question differently.

The words knew what they meant. But what they meant depended, in part, on who was listening.

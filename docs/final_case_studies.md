# Selected Case Studies for Final Presentation

## Selection Criteria

From 20 analyzed terms and 9 cross-cultural mappings, these 7 cases were chosen for the presentation. Each earns its place by illustrating a different aspect of the project's central claim: that the internal structure of Sanskrit words shapes philosophical meaning, and that meaning changes — sometimes subtly, sometimes dramatically — when it crosses into Chinese.

## The 7 Selected Cases

### Sanskrit Morphology Cases (4)

#### 1. samādhi — "A complete placing-together"
**Role in presentation**: Opening case and proof of method.
**Why selected**: Most important term for a meditation-focused class. The morphological breakdown (sam + ā + √dhā + -i) directly reveals the philosophical meaning — a total composing of awareness. Also corrects the common folk etymology (sama + ādhi = "equal placing"). This is the best term for showing the audience what the project's actual workflow looks like before moving into the more dramatic case studies.

#### 2. nirodha — "A complete holding-back"
**Role in presentation**: The "translation changes philosophy" example.
**Why selected**: Appears in the defining sutra (YS 1.2) so the audience already knows the word. The root √rudh ("to restrain") matters because different English translations ("cessation" vs. "suppression" vs. "stilling") carry very different connotations for practice. The Chinese 滅 ("extinction") introduces yet another interpretive layer — stronger than the Sanskrit warrants.

#### 3. abhiniveśa — "A deep settling-into"
**Role in presentation**: The "morphology matters" showcase.
**Why selected**: The triple-prefix structure (abhi + ni + √viś) physically enacts the concept — layers of "settling deeper and deeper in." Patañjali's observation that this clinging persists "even in the wise" (YS 2.9) is psychologically striking. This term demonstrates that Sanskrit word-building is not just linguistic trivia but a window into philosophical precision.

#### 4. saṃskāra — "A thorough making"
**Role in presentation**: The polysemy case. Shows how one word operates across multiple domains.
**Why selected**: Means "ritual consecration," "latent psychological trace," AND "volitional formation" depending on context. The word saṃskṛta ("refined") is the name of the Sanskrit language itself. This case demonstrates that Sanskrit terms are not static labels but living words whose meaning shifts with tradition.

### Cross-Cultural Mapping Cases (3)

#### 5. dhyāna → 禪那 → 禪 → chán → zen
**Role in presentation**: The "clear transmission" showcase. Strongest case in the project.
**Why selected**: The phonetic chain from Sanskrit to Japanese is one of the best-documented loanword paths in Asian languages. Four distinct Chinese renderings exist (禪那, 禪, 禪定, 靜慮), each representing a different translation strategy. An entire school of Buddhism takes its name from this word. No other term in the project carries this much transmission evidence.

#### 6. avidyā → 無明 — "Without brightness"
**Role in presentation**: The "translation as interpretation" case.
**Why selected**: 無明 is a structural calque (morphological mirror) of avidyā, but the Chinese translators chose "without brightness" over "without knowledge" — substituting a metaphor of darkness for a metaphor of ignorance. This is a small interpretive choice with real philosophical consequences: the Chinese image is more phenomenological (darkness), while the Sanskrit is more epistemological (not-knowing).

#### 7. svarūpa → 自性 — The cautionary case
**Role in presentation**: The "gap is more instructive than the connection" example.
**Why selected**: Shows the limits of mapping. In the Yoga Sutras, svarūpa ("own form") is what you discover when meditation succeeds — puruṣa's essential nature. In Chinese Mādhyamaka Buddhism, 自性 ("own nature") is what you discover *doesn't exist* — the central insight is emptiness of inherent nature. Same question, opposite answers. This case prevents the presentation from implying that cross-cultural transmission is smooth or lossless.

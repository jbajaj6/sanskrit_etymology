## 1. OPENING (~ 1 min)

I want to start with a sentence most of you already know:

> योगश्चित्तवृत्तिनिरोधः
>
> yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ
>
> "Yoga is the cessation of the fluctuations of the mind"

This is Yoga Sutra 1.2 — the definition of yoga itself. Most of us have encountered this sentence translated into English. But what I want to show you today is what happens when you look *inside* these Sanskrit words rather than just translating them. Because it turns out that the internal structure of these words — their roots, prefixes, and suffixes — is not just linguistic trivia. It's a compressed philosophical argument.

Three of the four key words in this sutra — citta, vṛtti, nirodha — are built from specific verbal roots and prefixes that tell you something precise about what the concept means. My project analyzed 20 of these terms from the Yoga Sutras and traced 9 of them into Chinese Buddhist translation to see what happened to them when they crossed cultures.

---

## 2. WHAT THE PROJECT STUDIES (~ 1 min)

The project does two things.

First, it takes Sanskrit philosophical terms — especially meditation vocabulary from the Yoga Sutras — and breaks them down into their component parts: the verbal root, the prefixes, the suffixes, the compound structure. The goal is to see how the literal, built-from-parts meaning relates to the philosophical meaning.

Second, for terms that entered Chinese through Buddhist translation, it asks: how were they rendered? Did the Chinese translators approximate the sound of the Sanskrit? Did they translate the meaning? And when they translated, what did they gain or lose?

I analyzed 20 terms in full and mapped 9 into Chinese. Today I'll walk through a few of the strongest cases.

## 3. HOW IT WORKS — samādhi (~ 1.5 min)

Let me start with samādhi, since it's the term most relevant to this class.

Samādhi is built from three parts:

- The root √dhā, which means "to place" or "to put"
- Two prefixes: sam- meaning "together" or "completely," and ā- meaning "toward"
- And the suffix -i, which makes it an action noun

So literally: sam + ā + √dhā + -i = "a complete placing-together." A total composing or settling of awareness.

Notice what the word's architecture is *doing*. It's not just naming a state — it's describing a process: the gathering and settling of attention into a unified whole. The word *argues* for what the experience is.

One quick sidebar: you'll sometimes hear people gloss samādhi as "equal-placing," from sama meaning "equal" plus ādhi. That's a folk etymology. Standard Sanskrit grammar derives it from the prefixes sam + ā plus the root √dhā. The dictionaries are clear on this. I mention it because this kind of correction is exactly what morphological analysis is good for — it keeps us honest about what words actually say.

## 4. CASE STUDIES — MORPHOLOGY (~ 3 min)

### nirodha — "A complete holding-back"

Now let's look at nirodha, the other crucial word in YS 1.2. Nirodha is built from the prefix ni- (meaning "down" or "back," here intensifying) plus the root √rudh, which means "to obstruct" or "to hold back," plus the suffix -a.

So literally: "a complete holding-back." A restraining. A stilling.

Why does this matter? Because different translators render nirodha differently in English, and the translation carries philosophical weight:

- "Cessation" — implies something ends
- "Suppression" — implies force
- "Stilling" — implies a quieting

The root √rudh — "to hold back" — is closer to "stilling" than to either "ending" or "forcing." Patañjali isn't saying the mind is destroyed. He's saying its habitual activity is held in check so that pure awareness can emerge.

This becomes even more interesting when we get to the Chinese. The Chinese rendering of Buddhist nirodha is 滅 — which means "to extinguish," like putting out a fire. That's *stronger* than the Sanskrit. It implies annihilation. And that gap — between "restraining" and "extinguishing" — is a real philosophical difference, not just a translation choice.

### abhiniveśa — "A deep settling-into"

This is my favorite case for showing why morphology matters. Abhiniveśa is the fifth kleśa — usually translated as "clinging to life" or "fear of death." It's built from:

- The root √viś, "to enter" or "to settle into"
- Two prefixes stacked on top of each other: abhi- ("toward, upon") and ni- ("down, into")
- Plus the suffix -a

Three layers of settling: you go *toward*, you go *down*, you *enter*. The morphology physically enacts what the concept describes — consciousness that has settled so deeply into the survival drive that it can't easily extract itself.

And Patañjali says something remarkable about it in YS 2.9: abhiniveśa "flows by its own momentum" and is "firmly established even in the wise." Even someone who intellectually understands that death is not to be feared still instinctively recoils. This is not an intellectual error. It's an embodied pattern — what we might today call a hardwired survival instinct. For a meditation practice, this names the deepest resistance you'll encounter: the organism's refusal to let go.

### saṃskāra — "A thorough making"

One more Sanskrit case, quickly. Saṃskāra — sam + √kṛ + -a — literally means "a thorough making" or "a complete preparation." It's from the same root that gives us the word "Sanskrit" itself: saṃskṛta means "refined" or "well-made."

But saṃskāra has three completely different lives depending on context. In ritual, it means "consecration" — the sacraments of life. In the Yoga Sutras, it means "latent impression" — the subliminal trace that every experience leaves in the mind. In Buddhist philosophy, it means "volitional formation" — the active constructive activity of the mind.

This polysemy matters because when saṃskāra entered Chinese as 行 (xíng), the translators captured the Buddhist sense — volitional action — but not the Yogic sense — latent trace. The Chinese word foregrounds *doing*; the Sanskrit (in Patañjali's usage) is about what has already been *done to you* and lies dormant. Same word, different traditions, different Chinese translation.

## 5. INDIA-TO-CHINA MAPPING (~ 2 min)

### dhyāna → 禪 → chán → zen

If I could show you only one mapping case, it would be this one.

The word dhyāna — "sustained meditation" — traveled from Sanskrit to Pali to Chinese to Japanese, and at each stage the pronunciation shifted:

dhyāna → jhāna → chánnà (禪那) → chán (禪) → zen

An entire school of Buddhism — Chan in Chinese, Zen in Japanese — takes its name from this single Sanskrit word. It's one of the best-documented loanword paths in all of Asian languages.

What's fascinating is that Chinese translators developed *four* different renderings: 禪那 (the full phonetic transliteration), 禪 (the abbreviated form), 禪定 (a hybrid pairing sound with meaning), and 靜慮 (Xuanzang's semantic translation, meaning "quiet contemplation"). Each one tells you something about how the concept was being processed.

And here's the doctrinal shift: in the Yoga Sutras, dhyāna is one specific stage of practice — limb 7 of 8. In Chinese Buddhism, 禪 expanded until it named an entire orientation toward awakening. The Chan school sometimes even de-emphasized the seated meditation that the word originally denoted. The word traveled, but its scope changed dramatically.

### avidyā → 無明

Avidyā is "non-knowledge" — a- ("not") plus vidyā ("knowledge"). The Chinese rendering is 無明 — 無 ("without") plus 明 ("brightness"). It's a structural mirror: the Chinese preserves the negative prefix and the abstract noun, almost piece by piece.

But the translators made a quiet interpretive choice. They chose "brightness" instead of "knowledge." The Sanskrit image is epistemological — a failure of knowing. The Chinese image is phenomenological — an absence of light. Darkness versus ignorance. Both are valid readings, but they feel different. A Chinese reader encounters obscuration; a Sanskrit reader encounters a mistake.

That's what I mean when I say translation is always interpretation — even when it's very faithful.

### svarūpa → 自性 — The cautionary case

Very briefly: svarūpa means "own form" — it's what puruṣa *is*, revealed when meditation succeeds. The closest Chinese candidate is 自性 — "own nature." Sounds similar, right?

But in Chinese Mādhyamaka Buddhism, 自性 is the thing that nothing *has*. The central insight of Mādhyamaka is svabhāva-śūnyatā — "emptiness of own-nature." The Yoga Sutras say you *find* your essential nature. Mādhyamaka says you *see through* the illusion that anything has one.

Same philosophical question. Opposite answers. The gap is the finding.

## 6. WHAT THIS REVEALS (~ 1 min)

Three things:

First, Sanskrit word-structure carries philosophical weight. Roots and prefixes are not decoration. When you break samādhi into "a complete placing-together," or nirodha into "a holding-back," or abhiniveśa into "a deep settling-into," you're not just doing linguistics. You're recovering an argument about what these concepts mean that the original language encodes and English translations flatten.

Second, translation is always interpretation. Every Chinese rendering made choices — sound or meaning, darkness or ignorance, extinction or restraint. Those choices shaped how millions of people understood these concepts. There is no neutral translation.

Third, cross-cultural transmission is real but partial. The dhyāna → zen connection is rock-solid. But the Yoga Sutras' specialized vocabulary — vṛtti, viveka-khyāti, kaivalya — has no Chinese counterpart at all. These are different traditions sharing a vocabulary, not a single tradition in two languages.

## 8. LIMITS (~ 30 sec)

A few honest caveats. The Yoga Sutras were never systematically translated into Chinese, so all the Chinese mappings are from Buddhist sources, not Yogic ones. Phonetic similarity between words is not proof of historical connection. And this project maps individual terms, not entire philosophical systems — the traditions have deep structural differences that no word-level analysis can capture.

Let me end where we started. Yoga Sutra 1.2:

yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ

After this project, I hear this sentence differently. Citta is not just "mind" — it's "the perceived," the accumulated field of consciousness. Vṛtti is not "thoughts" — it's "turnings," the ongoing revolution of mental activity. Nirodha is not "stopping" — it's "holding back," a quieting, not a destruction.

The words knew what they meant all along. We just had to learn how to listen to them.

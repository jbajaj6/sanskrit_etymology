## 2. WHAT THE PROJECT STUDIES (~ 1 min)

[Click to Slide 4 — How Sanskrit Word-Building Works.]

The project has two goals.

First, it takes Sanskrit philosophical terms — especially meditation vocabulary from the Yoga Sutras — and breaks them down into their component parts: the verbal root, the prefixes, the suffixes, the compound structure. The goal is to see how the literal, built-from-parts meaning relates to the philosophical meaning.

Second, for terms that entered Chinese through Buddhist translation, it asks: how were they rendered? Did the Chinese translators approximate the sound of the Sanskrit? Did they translate the meaning? And when they translated, what did they gain or lose?

I analyzed all of the 84 important terms from the Yoga Sutras we were given in class for these two goals.

## 3. HOW I ACTUALLY BROKE A WORD OPEN (~ 1.5 min)

How did I actually break a word open?

I used the same workflow each time -- using AI as a research assistant. One custom workflow forced the model to output the same structured fields every time: root, prefixes, suffixes, compound type, literal meaning, philosophical meaning, and key occurrences. A second workflow looked for possible Chinese Buddhist equivalents and tagged them as phonetic, semantic, hybrid, or only loosely related.

Then came the part that mattered: verification. I checked the Sanskrit side against standard dictionaries and grammatical tradition, and I checked the Chinese side against Buddhist text corpora and attested translation usage. So the AI helped me move faster across dozens of terms, but it did not get to make unsupported claims.

First, I started with the word in an actual textual setting — usually the sutra where it matters most — so that I wasn't treating it as an abstract dictionary object.

Second, I separated the visible parts: root, prefixes, suffixes, and, when necessary, compound members.

Third, I checked whether that segmentation was grammatically defensible.

Fourth, I reconstructed the literal meaning from the parts and compared that with the technical philosophical meaning in context.

And fifth, if the term had a Chinese afterlife, I asked a second set of questions: did Chinese translators preserve the sound, the meaning, or a mixture of both? And what changed when they did?

Basically, reverse-engineering each of these terms.

Let me show you quickly what that looks like with samādhi, since it's the term most relevant to this class.

Samādhi is built from three parts:

- The root √dhā, which means "to place" or "to put"
- Two prefixes: sam- meaning "together" or "completely," and ā- meaning "toward"
- And the suffix -i, which makes it an action noun

So literally: sam + ā + √dhā + -i = "a complete placing-together." A total composing or settling of awareness.

Samadhi is not just naming a state — it's describing a process: the gathering and settling of attention into a unified whole.

That, in miniature, is the whole project: isolate the load-bearing parts of the word, test the breakdown, and then ask what philosophical work that structure is doing.

## 4. CASE STUDIES — MORPHOLOGY (~ 3 min)

[Next Slide]

### nirodha — "A complete holding-back"

Now let's look at nirodha, the other crucial word in 1.2. Nirodha is built from the prefix ni- (meaning "down" or "back," here it's actually an intensifier) plus the root √rodh, which means "to obstruct" or "to hold back," plus the suffix -a.

So literally: "a complete holding-back." Kind of like restraining.

Why does this matter? Because different translators render nirodha differently in English, and the translation carries philosophical weight:

- "Cessation" — implies something ends
- "Suppression" — implies force
- "Stilling" — implies a quieting

The root √rudh — "to hold back" — is closer to "stilling" than to either "ending" or "forcing." 

This becomes even more interesting when we get to the Chinese. The Chinese rendering of Buddhist nirodha is myeh 滅 — which means "to extinguish," like putting out a fire. That's *stronger* than the Sanskrit. It implies annihilation. And that gap — between "restraining" and "extinguishing" — is a real philosophical difference, not just a translation choice.

[Click to Slide 7 — abhiniveśa.]

### abhiniveśa — "A deep settling-into"

This is my favorite case for showing why morphology matters. Abhiniveśa is the fifth kleśa — usually translated as "clinging to life" or "fear of death." It's built from:

- The root √viś, "to enter" or "to settle into"
- Two prefixes stacked on top of each other: abhi- ("toward, upon") and ni- ("down, into")
- Plus the suffix -a

Three layers of settling: you go *toward*, you go *down*, you *enter*. The morphology physically enacts what the concept describes — consciousness that has settled so deeply into the survival, instinctual drive that it can't easily extract itself.

In sutra 2.9: abhiniveśa "flows by its own momentum" and is "firmly established even in the wise." Even someone who intellectually understands that death is not to be feared still instinctively recoils. This is not an intellectual error. It's an embodied pattern — what we might today call a hardwired survival instinct. For a meditation practice, this names the deepest resistance you'll encounter: the organism's refusal to let go.

[Click to Slide 8 — saṃskāra.]

### saṃskāra — "A thorough making"

Saṃskāra — sam + √kṛ + -a — literally means "a thorough making" or "a complete preparation." It's from the same root that gives us the word "Sanskrit" itself: saṃskṛta means "refined" or "well-made."

But saṃskāra has three completely different lives depending on context. In ritual, it means "consecration" — the sacraments of life. In the Yoga Sutras, it means "latent impression" — the subliminal trace that every experience leaves in the mind. In Buddhist philosophy, it means "volitional formation" — the active constructive activity of the mind.

This matters because when saṃskāra entered Chinese as 行 (shing), the translators captured the Buddhist sense — volitional action — but not the Yogic sense — latent trace. The Chinese word foregrounds *doing*; the Sanskrit is about what has already been *done to you* and lies dormant. Same word, different traditions, different Chinese translation.

## 5. INDIA-TO-CHINA MAPPING (~ 2 min)

[Click to Slide 9 — When Concepts Cross Languages. Pause for one beat before speaking.]

So far I've shown the Sanskrit side: the internal structure of the words. Now I want to show what happened when some of those concepts crossed from India into Chinese Buddhist translation.

[Click to Slide 10 — dhyāna to chan to zen.]

### dhyāna → 禪 → chán → zen

If I could show you only one mapping case, it would be this one. Professor, has already shown it to us, but I would like to reiterate it.

The word dhyāna — "sustained meditation" — traveled from Sanskrit to Pali to Chinese to Japanese, and at each stage the pronunciation shifted:

dhyāna → jhāna → chánnà (禪那) → chán (禪) → zen

An entire school of Buddhism — Chan in Chinese, Zen in Japanese — takes its name from this single Sanskrit word. It's one of the best-documented loanword paths in all of Asian languages.

What's fascinating is that Chinese translators developed *four* different renderings: 禪那 (the full phonetic transliteration), 禪 (the abbreviated form), 禪定 (a hybrid pairing sound with meaning), and 靜慮 (Xuanzang's semantic translation, meaning "quiet contemplation"). Each one tells you something about how the concept was being processed.

And here's the doctrinal shift: in the Yoga Sutras, dhyāna is one specific stage of practice — limb 7 of 8. In Chinese Buddhism, 禪 expanded until it named an entire orientation toward awakening. The Chan school sometimes even de-emphasized the seated meditation that the word originally denoted. The word traveled, but its scope changed dramatically.

[Click to Slide 11 — avidyā to 無明.]

### avidyā → 無明

Avidyā is "non-knowledge" — a- ("not") plus vidyā ("knowledge"). The Chinese rendering is woo-ming — woo 無 ("without") plus ming 明 ("brightness"). It's a structural mirror: the Chinese preserves the negative prefix and the abstract noun, almost piece by piece.

But the translators made a quiet interpretive choice. They chose "brightness" instead of "knowledge." The Sanskrit image is epistemological — a failure of knowing. The Chinese image is phenomenological — an absence of light. Darkness versus ignorance. Both are valid readings, but they feel different. A Chinese reader encounters obscuration; a Sanskrit reader encounters a mistake.

That's what I mean when I say translation is always interpretation — even when it's very faithful.
